## Strategic Profile: Anthropic (creator of Claude)

---

### time_horizon

Anthropic has demonstrably accepted short-term commercial disadvantage in the name of long-term positioning. The company maintained a restricted API access program for over a year after Claude's initial release, limiting scale to manage deployment risk — a direct revenue cost. Anthropic has published multi-year safety research agendas (Constitutional AI, Responsible Scaling Policy with defined ASL tiers) that include explicit commitments to pause or restrict deployment if capability evaluations cross defined thresholds. The RSP is notable precisely because it attempts to bind future behavior, not just describe current practice — though critics reasonably note enforcement remains self-administered. The company was founded explicitly as a spin-out from OpenAI over disagreements about deployment pace, and its long-term benefit corporation structure and unusual "public benefit" equity arrangements with investors signal genuine, if imperfect, institutional commitment to non-short-term optimization.

**Score: 72**

---

### transparency_threshold

Anthropic publishes model cards and system prompt policies, and has released detailed technical papers on Constitutional AI and RLHF methodology. However, it does not publish training data provenance in any meaningful detail, does not release red-team reports with operational specificity (findings are summarized at a high level), and capability evaluations shared publicly are selective rather than comprehensive. The RSP publishes evaluation *frameworks* but not raw evaluation results or internal safety incident disclosures. Relative to the theoretical maximum, Anthropic is meaningfully more transparent than most frontier labs on methodology but significantly less transparent than the 100-end on data sourcing, internal audits, and incident reporting. The jurisdictional ceiling imposed by U.S. national security norms (NSPM-33, export control obligations, potential classification of dual-use capability findings) further constrains what can be disclosed.

**Score: 52**

---

### risk_tolerance

Anthropic applies genuine gatekeeping: tiered API access, use-case restrictions, operator accountability frameworks, and explicit refusal categories baked into deployment. Claude models have shipped with more conservative default behaviors than comparable frontier models from OpenAI or Google at equivalent capability levels. However, Anthropic does deploy frontier models commercially within months of training completion — it is not withholding capabilities for years pending comprehensive external safety review. The company has released increasingly capable models (Claude 2, Claude 3 family, Claude 3.5, Claude 3.7) on a roughly annual cadence competitive with industry peers, indicating that commercial deployment pressure is real and operative. The risk tolerance is meaningfully below the industry median but not at the extreme low end.

**Score: 32**

---

### democratic_tendency

Anthropic has not released open-weights models and has actively avoided doing so, citing safety concerns about uncontrolled proliferation — a position that functionally centralizes capability access. Its API licensing terms are restrictive, prohibiting competitive model training and a range of use cases. In AI governance forums and Congressional testimony, Anthropic executives have supported licensing regimes and compute thresholds that, while framed as safety measures, would structurally advantage well-capitalized incumbents over new entrants. The company has not publicly opposed regulatory frameworks that would raise barriers to entry. These actions place Anthropic toward the centralizing end of the spectrum despite its stated commitment to broad benefit — the institutional incentives and policy positions are more consistent with managed access than distributed capability. The U.S. macro ceiling of 70 is not a binding constraint here; Anthropic's own choices drive the score below it.

**Score: 34**
